Syntax

There are five basic strokes:

  • h for horizontal
  • v for vertical
  • u for upward diagonal
  • d for downward diagonal
  • c for the Winkelhaken

There are three basic ways of combining elements:

  • [] stacks things next to each other horizontally
  • {} stacks things on top of each other vertically
  • () makes things cross/overlap

These are used recursively to build up more complicated signs.

Examples

  • u is a single Winkelhaken: c
  • me is a vertical next to a horizontal: [vh]
  • pa is a vertical crossing a stack of two horizontals: (v{hh})

Aesthetics

There are a few special ways to modify the look of a sign, though these are ignored when searching:

  • The letters S, P, L, or W at the beginning of the sign change the shape of the canvas to Square, Portrait, Landscape, or Wide
  • 2 after a stroke doubles its head
  • ' after a stroke shortens it from the head end; " shortens it from the tail end
  • Since Winkelhaken don't have heads or tails, ' and " just make them smaller
  • T after a component rotates it 45 degrees (T for tenû, the Akkadian term, or just for "tilted")
  • E after a component asks the spacing algorithm to give it additional space, if possible (E for "expand")
  • M after a component puts a small margin around it, separating it from other elements
  • 0 (the number zero) creates a "void", which takes up space but doesn't appear on the screen
  • 0' is a "vertical void" that takes up no horizontal space; 0" is a "horizontal void" that takes up no vertical space; 0'" takes up no space at all but prevents things from kerning together
  • ! after a single stroke turns it green, which…might be useful for demonstration purposes? This one's really just a side effect of other features but it might as well be documented

Search tips

  • Winkelhaken and small downward diagonals often look very similar. If you're not finding anything, try swapping one for the other.
  • Ancient scribes didn't always distinguish downward-over-upward and downward-over-horizontal as well as they should. If you can't find your sign with one, try the other.
  • Use () sparingly; if it's not clear that the strokes are actually intended to cross, it should probably be [] or {} instead.
  • The tenû modifier is ignored when searching, so only use it when an entire structure is tilted; if it's only a couple strokes, encode them as downward and upward diagonals instead.
  • You can use a star * to indicate that something should be in a certain position, but it doesn't matter what.
