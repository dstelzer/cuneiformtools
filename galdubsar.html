What is this?

This is the shiny new-and-improved version of my DUB.SAR tool and a spiritual successor to Cuneify. Give it the transliteration of a Hittite text, and it'll render it in cuneiform. Unlike Cuneify, the result is an image (so you don't need to have any fonts installed), and unlike DUB.SAR, it uses my recursive graphical encoding rather than drawings of the signs.

How do I use it?

Type a transliteration into the box, then hit "Render". For the most part, this follows standard Hethitological conventions: try Hrozný's famous example
nu NINDA-an e-ez-za-at-te-ni
wa-a-tar-ma e-ku-ut-te-ni

What notation does this use?

Uppercase versus lowercase is ignored. Dots and hyphens separate signs. Spaces (or `w) separate words. Newlines (or `n) separate lines. `r makes a ruling (a horizontal line across the whole tablet). `f or `F makes a "fill" that will expand to take up any available space—Hittite scribes liked their text to be justified to both margins, but they didn't have the benefit of my algorithms, so you can use `f to e.g. ensure that all excess space is put right before the last sign, or `F (which hatches the space) to show that a significant part of the line is damaged and unreadable.

All standard sign names should be recognized, including shorthand like "m" for the determinative DIŠ (*). Separate all signs with hyphens or dots, including determinatives and punctuation: d-U for dU. Use a slash and a number after a sign name to specify a variant (like ni/2 for the second variant of NI). This works for punctuation too: : is the first type of Glossenkeil, :/2 is the second type, etc. If you want to include a custom sign, use a percent sign followed by a recursive code (like %{hvh}).

Even though they're not distinct in Hittite, it's important to distinguish S and Š for the sake of Sumerograms and Akkadograms—SI "horn" is a different sign from ŠI "eye". For the sake of easier typing, ASCII replacements are understood:

  • C → Š
  • SZ → Š
  • S, → Ṣ
  • T, → Ṭ
  • J → Ŋ
  • H → Ḫ
Similarly, you can use normal numbers instead of subscripts or accents (LU2 for LÚ or LU₂), and x instead of a multiplication sign (SIxSA for SI×SA).

(*) The shorthand determinatives are m (DIŠ), f (MUNUS), d (DIŊIR), and g (GIŠ/ŊIŠ). Some publishers abbreviate DIŠ as I (the Roman numeral for 1), but that's also the name of a totally different sign, so it's ambiguous; use 1 instead.
