In some cases the HZL assigns the same reading to multiple signs, if there's no semantic difference between them. For example, GÍN "shekel" is written with sign #209B in Boğazköy, but sign #223 in letters from Egypt. Since they mean exactly the same thing, it's not generally a problem to transcribe them both as GÍN. Similarly, the compound logogram BANŠUR "table" is sometimes written as URU.URUDU, sometimes as E.URUDU, with no difference in meaning. For most purposes, this is no different from giving both the Old Script and the New Script versions of #343 the reading LI.

But since this database is also used for the GAL.DUB.SAR typesetting tool, it's necessary that each reading correspond to exactly one sign—not just semantically, but in terms of sign index numbers. Thus, we read #223 as GÍN and #209B as GIN₉, URU.URUDU as BANŠUR and E.URUDU as BANŠUR₄.

For the same reason, graphic ligatures—when adjacent signs overlap, without any special semantic meaning—are given their own names and entries in the database, even if they don't have their own HZL numbers. These are generally named with a + between the components (the ligature of A and NA is called A+NA), and this is how they can be selected in GAL.DUB.SAR.
