Ḫantatallaš

One of the biggest difficulties in working with cuneiform is recognizing the signs. There's no easy way to look up a sign by its shape, so while it's easy to go from a pronunciation to a visual form (like my DUB.SAR tool does), that's no help when you're actually looking at the clay.

As an experiment, I've been working on a "graphical encoding" for cuneiform. It's a way to encode a cuneiform glyph's shape, rather than its meaning; eventually, the goal is to use this as a sort of index, letting people look up an unknown sign without needing to search through the entire Zeichenlexikon.

The main drawback of the encoding is that it's much harder to read than it is to write. And that's what this tool is meant to help with. Put an encoded sign into the text box at the top, choose the style you want, and it'll draw it out graphically.

NOTE: An experimental, more user-friendly version of this tool is now available. We recommend using that page instead. You can also search the Zeichenlexikon directly.

The encoding

There are five basic strokes:

  • h for horizontal
  • v for vertical
  • u for upward diagonal
  • d for downward diagonal
  • c for the Winkelhaken
So for example, u is encoded as c, and aš is encoded as h.

These can be combined in three different ways:

  • [] stacks things next to each other horizontally
  • {} stacks things on top of each other vertically
  • () makes two things cross
Thus, me is [vh] (a vertical next to a horizontal), and nu is (ch) (a horizontal crossing a Winkelhaken).

These can be combined recursively to create more complicated signs. For example, pa is (v{hh}) (a stack of two horizontals, crossing a vertical), and ti is {v[hc]c} (a vertical, on top of a horizontal-next-to-a-Winkelhaken, on top of a Winkelhaken).

Finally, there are a few special ways to modify strokes and groups aesthetically. These are mostly ignored in the searching system; I just use them to make the glyphs look better.

  • S, P, L, or W at the very beginning changes the size of the canvas: square, portrait, landscape, or wide (square is the default)
  • 2 after a stroke doubles its head, as in an: (h2v) (in searching, a double-headed stroke is treated as a group of two strokes)
  • ' or " after a stroke makes it shorter: a single quote shortens it from the head end, a double quote shortens it from the tail end
  • ' and/or " after a Winkelhaken make it smaller, as in bi {[hc'][hc']}
  • T after a component tilts it 45 degrees, e.g. ḫi {[vvv]v}T (T for tenû, the Akkadian term for tilted signs…or just for "tilt" if you prefer)
  • E after a component tells the renderer to give it more space (E for "expand")
  • M after a component tells the renderer to leave a small margin on all sides of it; this is intended especially for signs contained within other signs, where there's a distinct gap between the inner and outer signs (M for "margin")
  • 0 (the number zero, not the letter O) creates a "void", which takes up space but doesn't appear in the image; 0' is a vertical-only void, 0" is a horizontal-only void, 0'" is a void that takes up no space at all (that is, a "zero-width non-joiner")
  • ! after a single stroke turns it green, which…might be useful for demonstration purposes? This one's really a side effect of other features but it might as well be documented

Here are some more examples of signs laid out in this notation:

  • a = P[vv2]
  • ši = [cvh]
  • iš = L[{0[hc]h}vv]
  • ni = L{d(u[0vv0])}
  • ar = W[cvh2vvcv]
  • ba = [{hh'h}v]
  • wi5 = L[{0[hc]h}v{0dh}]

If you want to test the searching system, type in a part of a sign here, and it will give you a list of signs that contain that part.

Tips for effective searching:

  • Winkelhaken and downward diagonals often look very similar
  • Scribes don't always distinguish downward-over-upward and downward-over-horizontal as well as they should; if you can't find your sign with one, try the other
  • Use () sparingly; if it's not clear that the strokes are meant to cross, it's likely [] or {} instead
  • The tenû modifier is ignored when searching, so only use it when an entire structure is tilted; if it's only a couple strokes, encode them as downward and upward diagonals instead
  • You can use a star * to indicate that something should be in a certain position, but it doesn't matter what
